About me

I am a Postdoctoral Associate in Applied Psychology at New York University, working with Catherine Tamis-LeMonda. I received my Ph.D. in Communicative Sciences and Disorders from NYU, where I worked with Sudha Arunachalam.

My research examines how children learn and use language across contexts and cultures. I study early vocabulary development, bilingual language learning, narrative skills, and the ways social and multisensory experiences support language growth.

Research

My primary interest is understanding how infants and young children build language from the input and interactions available in everyday life. I combine experimental approaches with naturalistic observation, caregiver report, and language-sample analysis.

A central strand of my work focuses on bilingual assessment. With colleagues, I developed the Dual Language Learners English-Mandarin (DLL-EM) Inventories, culturally responsive vocabulary measures with matched concepts across English and Mandarin.

My current research centers on three goals. First, to understand how multisensory experiences support early vocabulary development. Second, to develop equitable tools for assessing bilingual children. Third, to identify how linguistic structure, cognitive load, and elicitation context shape language learning and production.
